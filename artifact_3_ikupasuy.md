# Artifact 3 — Ikupasuy (Prayer Stick / Libation Stick)

**Category:** Ceremonial tool
**Material:** Carved wood (approximately 20–30 cm)
**Exhibit Role:** The pivot — from "what the Ainu believed" to "what was banned"

---

The ikupasuy is a flat wooden prayer stick — roughly 20–30 centimeters long, carved with the flowing geometric patterns found throughout Ainu art. At every meal and every ceremony, it was dipped into a cup of sake or millet wine and lifted toward the *kamuy* (divine spirits) as an offering and prayer. It was the Ainu household's daily telephone to God — intimate, specific, repeated throughout a lifetime.

In 1899, the Japanese Meiji government passed the Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act, which banned Ainu religious practice. The ikupasuy became illegal. Families who continued praying with it did so in secret — hiding this small carved stick from a government that had decided their daily conversation with the divine should stop.

This is the pivot of our exhibit: the shift from what the Ainu *believed* to what was *banned*.
