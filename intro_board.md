# The Thread That Was Almost Cut: How Ainu Culture Survived

## Introduction Board

For thousands of years, before Japan was a modern nation, before Hokkaido was a tourist destination, before Sapporo was a city, another civilization called this land home. They were the Ainu — the indigenous people of what is now northern Japan, living across *Ainu Mosir* (the "land of the humans"): Hokkaido, the Kuril Islands, and the island of Sakhalin. At their height in the 18th century, roughly 80,000 Ainu peopled these territories. Today, official counts number only around 11,450 — though the true population of people with Ainu ancestry may be ten to twenty times higher, hidden by generations of forced assimilation.

The Ainu built their world on a single, profound idea: nothing in nature is ordinary. Every bear, every salmon, every river, every flame carries a *kamuy* — a divine spirit inhabiting a physical form to bring gifts to the human world. The Ainu understood themselves to be in a sacred contract with these spirits. Nature provides food, shelter, and life; humans return respect, gratitude, and ceremony. This was not superstition. It was theology, ecology, law, and identity woven into one unbreakable thread. When an Ainu elder dipped a carved prayer stick into sake and gestured toward the hearth fire, he was not performing a ritual. He was fulfilling an obligation to the divine world that had kept his community alive.

Then, in 1899, the Meiji government of Japan passed the Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act. The word "former" was not accidental. It told a story — that the Ainu had already ceased to be a distinct people and needed only to be guided into a Japanese agricultural future. Under that law, Ainu land was confiscated and redistributed to Japanese settlers. The Ainu language was banned from schools. Religious ceremonies, including the great bear-sending ceremony *Iyomante*, were outlawed as "barbaric." Ainu people were expected to stop being Ainu. That law remained in force for nearly a century.

What came close to being lost in those hundred years is difficult to overstate. The Ainu language — which had no written form and carried an entire civilization's history, ethics, and cosmology inside its sounds — fell from the lips of thousands to the lips of almost no one. The *yukar*, the epic oral poems sung for hours, sometimes days, from the point of view of the gods themselves, could no longer be performed in their original form. Each elder who died took irreplaceable knowledge with them.

This exhibit tells that story of near-loss and survival through five objects. A bear skull from the *Iyomante* ceremony. An elm-bark robe whose geometric patterns are a form of prayer. A prayer stick that a government banned. A manuscript completed by a 19-year-old Ainu woman who spent her short life writing down the epics before they could vanish — and who died just months before her book was published. And a musical instrument, nearly extinct, that a contemporary Ainu musician now plays on stages around the world. Each object is a thread. Together, they weave the answer to one question: *when a government tries to erase a people's story, what happens?*

The Ainu are still here. In 2019, Japan officially recognized the Ainu as an indigenous people in national law for the first time. In 2020, the National Ainu Museum — *Upopoy*, meaning "singing together in a large group" — opened in Hokkaido. Craftswomen are reviving bark-fiber weaving. Young people are learning the language from recordings. OKI, an Ainu musician, tours internationally with a five-stringed zither that was nearly forgotten. The thread was almost cut. It was not.
