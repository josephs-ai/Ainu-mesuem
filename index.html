How the Ainu Carved the Forest into Spiraling Messages for Their Gods

The Ainu turned the forests of Hokkaido into prayers — carving wood into spiraling messages for the gods who lived inside every tree, river, and flame.

Introduction

Where the Forest Speaks

Look at the five objects in this room. A ceremonial robe. A prayer stick. A knife sheath. A headband. A carved wand. They come from different hands, different decades, different purposes. But if you look closely — really look — you'll find the same shape on every single one. A curl. A spiral. A line that coils back on itself like a wave frozen mid-break.

That shape has a name: moreu. And it is the most important thing in this room.

The Ainu are the Indigenous people of Hokkaido, the large northern island of Japan. For thousands of years, they lived in deep forest — hunting bear and deer, fishing rivers choked with salmon, reading the land the way others read books. They didn't divide the world into sacred and ordinary. To them, everything alive carried a spirit, a kamuy: the fire, the water, the bear, the owl, the mountain. The forest wasn't scenery. It was congregation. Every tree a god standing still.

When an Ainu craftsman made a prayer tool, he started with a stick. He didn't carve it into a new shape — he shaved it, peeling back thin curls of wood so they spiraled upward from the shaft like smoke rising, or like a fern uncurling in spring. These were inaw — prayer sticks — and the shavings didn't fall away. They stayed attached, coiling outward, becoming the prayer itself. Wood becoming curl. Material becoming spirit.

That curl — that moreu — moved from the inaw onto everything the Ainu touched. Women stitched it in white thread on dark robes, working the spiral into bands of embroidery that ran down sleeves and hems. Men cut it into knife sheaths and headbands. The same shape, repeated until it was no longer decoration. It was language. It was identity. It was proof that the world spirals back toward itself, that nothing ends in a straight line.

In 1869, Japan annexed Hokkaido. What followed was systematic erasure. Ainu land was seized and redistributed to Japanese settlers. Children were sent to schools where speaking Ainu meant punishment. The great bear ceremony, Iomante, was banned in 1955. Artifacts were confiscated. Some were destroyed. Some ended up in foreign museums, labeled primitive and exotic, stripped of their names.

Japan tried to make the Ainu disappear. And it nearly worked.

Today, fewer than ten native speakers of the Ainu language remain. The traditional hunting grounds are gone. The ceremonies are gone. The bear rituals are gone. By almost every measure used by linguists and anthropologists, the Ainu are one of the closest cases of cultural extinction in the modern world. A people who lived for ten thousand years reduced, in barely a century, to a paragraph in someone else's history book.

But here is what cultural extinction cannot do: it cannot reach inside a hand.

Any Ainu woman with a needle and thread can stitch the moreu onto a new cloth. Any Ainu man with a blade can shave a fresh curl onto a plain stick. You can burn the robe. You can break the prayer stick. You can clear-cut the forest until there is nothing left but stumps. But you cannot confiscate muscle memory. You cannot arrest the knowledge that lives in fingers.

The moreu survived because it never lived in objects. It lived in people.

In 2019, Japan finally recognized the Ainu as an Indigenous people — 150 years late. In 2020, the Upopoy National Ainu Museum opened in Hokkaido. The spiral is still being stitched. Still being shaved. Still coiling upward from new hands into new cloth.

The five artifacts in this room all carry that same curl. Follow it from case to case. Notice where it appears. Notice that it's always the same shape — but never quite the same form. On wood, the moreu rises as a physical curl. On cloth, it flattens into geometric thread. On a carved surface, it becomes a shadow in a groove. The Ainu adapted their sacred spiral to every material the forest gave them, and every adaptation was still a prayer.

Forest into prayer. Hands into history.

The spiral was never lost. It was just waiting.

01

Inaw

イナウ  —  The Whittled Messenger

Carved from wood and coated in red and black lacquer, this small prayer stick displays bold painted moreu spirals — concentric circles flowing into sweeping bands across its flat surface. Among the Ainu, inaw were sacred offerings crafted by drawing a specialized blade, the inawke-makiri, along the bark to peel curling ribbons that spiraled upward from the wood (Fitzhugh and Dubreuil). Each species carried specific spiritual intent: willow summoned benevolent kamuy, while alder repelled malevolent forces ("Inau"). Placed at hearths during daily prayer, at fishing grounds before harvests, and beside the body at births and burials, inaw served as the primary channel between the human and divine worlds. By design, they were always temporary — when the wood softened and the shaved ribbons finally dropped away, the Ainu understood the prayer had been received and the message carried to the gods (Utagawa).

02

Attush

アットゥシ  —  The Bark-Cloth Robe

Woven by Ainu women in the 19th century, this attush robe features a golden-brown body of ohyo elm bark fiber overlaid with dark indigo cotton appliqué and pale blue chain-stitch embroidery. Bold geometric patterns derived from moreu spirals run symmetrically down the front panels and across the sleeves. To produce the textile, women harvested inner bark of the ohyo elm, soaked and split the fibers over weeks, then wove them on backstrap looms (Fitzhugh and Dubreuil). The moreu-derived motifs positioned along the collar, cuffs, hem, and back panel were not decorative but spiritually functional — placed at every vulnerable opening where Ainu believed evil forces could enter through unguarded seams, the patterns invoked ancestor spirits to shield the wearer (Hunger). Beyond spiritual armor, attush robes served as valued trade goods prized by Japanese merchants for their durability, transforming the sacred spiral from three-dimensional wood curl into flattened thread ("Ainu Culture").

03

Ikupasuy

イクパスイ  —  The Prayer Stick

Carved from a single piece of light-brown wood, this ikupasuy features an openwork split at its upper end and delicate leaf-shaped moreu motifs spiraling in shallow incision down the shaft. Used by the Ainu as a permanent prayer wand, the ikupasuy served as a lifelong channel between its owner and the kamuy. During ceremony, the worshipper dipped the pointed "tongue" end into sake or millet beer, then flicked drops toward the sacred hearth fire to carry offerings to the divine realm (Fitzhugh and Dubreuil). Each wand bore moreu designs unique to the owner's patrilineal clan, ensuring the kamuy recognized who was praying ("Ikupasuy"). Before ritual use, the ikupasuy was passed through the rorun-puyar, the sacred eastern window, to sanctify it. Unlike the inaw, which dissolves after use, the ikupasuy endured across generations as a permanent vessel of prayer and ancestral identity (Utagawa).

The blade that carves the prayer carries one of its own…

04

Makiri

マキリ  —  The Carving Knife

Carried by every Ainu adult from childhood to death, this wooden makiri sheath features deeply carved moreu spirals across its upper half — bold interlocking coils set against crosshatched diamond fill — with a braided vegetable-fiber cord threaded through the top for wearing at the waist. Its lower half, worn smooth from generations of use, speaks to the knife's constant presence in Ainu life (Fitzhugh and Dubreuil). Sheaths bearing such elaborate moreu were courtship gifts, their intricate patterns carved over months as declarations of devotion ("Ainu Culture"). Beyond utilitarian and romantic functions, a sacred variant called the inawke-makiri served as the ritual blade for shaving willow branches into inaw prayer sticks — offerings that carried human petitions to the spirit world. The makiri thus bridged the everyday and the divine: the same tool that processed the forest's bounty also transformed it into prayer ("Inau").

The spiral rises to the brow…

05

Matanpushi

マタンプシ  —  The Headband

Fashioned from black cotton and trade cloth, this matanpushi headband widens into a distinctive eye-shaped center panel where white chain-stitch embroidery traces bold moreu spirals — two prominent coils flanked by thorned, barbed curves branching toward the temples, with black ties accented by brown and tan cotton at each end. Ainu women served as spiritual guardians of their households, and the matanpushi placed these protective spirals directly over the forehead, the precise point where harmful spirits were believed to enter the body (Fitzhugh and Dubreuil). Each stitch functioned as prayer, with mothers passing the craft to daughters across generations (Hunger). After Japan's annexation of Hokkaido and the forced assimilation campaigns that followed, many women could no longer wear the headband openly, yet the stitching continued in private, transforming the matanpushi into a quiet act of resistance preserving Ainu identity through needle and thread (Morris-Suzuki).
