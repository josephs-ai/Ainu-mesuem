# Artifact 2 — Attush Robe (Elm Bark Fiber Ceremonial Garment)

The **attush** is a full-length robe woven from the inner bark of the Japanese elm — a tree the Ainu call *ohyo*. Women stripped the bark, softened the fibers, and wove them into cloth, then embroidered the surface with flowing dark-blue geometric spirals known as *Ainu mon'yō*. These patterns weren't decoration. They were believed to ward off evil spirits — meaning every Ainu person literally wore spiritual protection on their body.

The attush was used for both daily life and ceremony, making it one of the most visible expressions of Ainu identity. That's precisely why Japan's 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act targeted it: pressuring Ainu to adopt Japanese dress was a deliberate attempt to make an entire people invisible.

Today, Ainu craftswomen are reviving bark-fiber weaving. The robe that colonialism tried to erase is being rewoven, stitch by stitch.
