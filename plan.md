### PROPOSAL A: "The Sacred Contract — Ainu Spirituality and the World of Kamuy"

**Narrative Angle:** Go deep on Ainu cosmology. The entire Ainu worldview is built on a single idea: everything in nature is inhabited by divine spirits called *kamuy*, and humans survive by honoring a sacred reciprocal contract with them. This is not just religion — it is ethics, ecology, identity, and law all at once. Show five objects that each represent one facet of that contract.

**Story Arc:** The exhibit opens by asking: *what did the Ainu believe?* Each artifact adds one layer — the spiritual object (inau), the prayer medium (ikupasuy), the great ceremony (bear skull from Iyomante), the daily embodiment of those beliefs in clothing (attush robe), and finally the sacred sound of music that is itself a form of prayer (mukkuri jaw harp).

**Artifacts:**

1. **Inau** (ceremonial wooden shavings) — *Ceremonial object.* The most fundamental Ainu sacred object. A shaved willow or birch stick whose curling ribbons remain attached — offered at every ceremony, placed at hearths, graves, and riversides. Explains what "prayer" looks like in Ainu practice.

2. **Ikupasuy** (prayer stick / libation stick) — *Ceremonial tool.* Flat carved wooden stick, finely incised with geometric patterns. Dipped in sake and used by the head of household to gesture offerings toward the kamuy. The human "telephone" to the divine world. Visually beautiful; easy to find museum images of.

3. **Bear skull from Iyomante** — *Ceremonial object.* The climactic object of the most elaborate Ainu ceremony. The bear is raised as a divine guest, ritually killed to "send its spirit home," then its skull is mounted on the sacred nusa fence surrounded by inau. Extremely well-documented and visually powerful. Directly ties to the Kim-un Kamuy (bear god) narrative.

4. **Attush robe** — *Clothing.* Bark-fiber robe with embroidered geometric patterns (Ainu mon'yō). The patterns aren't purely decorative — they are believed to protect the wearer from evil spirits. The clothing itself is a physical prayer. Housed in the British Museum and National Ainu Museum; extensively photographed.

5. **Mukkuri** (jaw harp) — *Instrument.* Small bamboo instrument played primarily by women. Because "almost every type of Ainu song is sacred," even a humble jaw harp is imbued with spiritual meaning. Its intimate sound-making process (the mouth as a resonance box) represents how the sacred pervades the ordinary.

**Why these five work together:** Every object is a different *medium* for the same message — the kamuy relationship. The progression goes from communal ceremony (inau, bear skull) to household prayer (ikupasuy) to worn identity (attush) to personal musical prayer (mukkuri). There's no redundancy in what story each tells.

**Student oral exam confidence:** The Iyomante ceremony is memorable, dramatic, and extensively documented. A student can explain the kamuy concept once and then show how it flows through every artifact. The through-line is always "this is how the Ainu honored their contract with the divine."

### PROPOSAL B: "The Thread That Was Almost Cut — Ainu Culture Under Colonial Attack"

**Narrative Angle:** Breadth across the arc of colonialism and survival. The Ainu had a rich, complete culture — and then the Japanese Meiji government tried to erase it. This exhibit doesn't just show what the culture *was*; it shows what was almost destroyed and what survived. The artifacts build from cultural richness → targeted suppression → near-extinction → desperate preservation → ongoing revival.

**Story Arc:** Start with what the Ainu built (the attush robe as living culture), move to what colonialism tried to destroy (ikupasuy — specifically banned in 1899), show what almost vanished (Yukie Chiri's manuscript — the language preserved at the last possible moment), and end with what is being reclaimed (the tonkori and OKI's revival). The bear skull anchors the spiritual worldview at the beginning, grounding *why* its suppression mattered.

**Artifacts:**

1. **Bear skull from Iyomante** — *Ceremonial object.* Opens by establishing the living spiritual world — this is what the Ainu believed, what they practiced, and what the Meiji government banned as "barbaric." Sets up everything that follows.

2. **Attush robe** — *Clothing.* The visual signature of Ainu culture — and still being woven today by revivalist craftswomen. Shows that Ainu material culture is not frozen in the past.

3. **Ikupasuy** (prayer stick) — *Ceremonial tool.* Directly connects to the 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act, which banned the use of prayer sticks and other religious practices as part of forced assimilation. The ikupasuy IS the religious practice that the colonial law tried to eliminate.

4. **Yukie Chiri's 1922 manuscript / *Ainu shin'yoshū*** — *Written/recorded material.* A 19-year-old Ainu woman transcribed and translated Ainu oral epic poetry into Japanese before dying at age 19 — racing against the extinction of her own language. A real published document. Her story is as powerful as any artifact.

5. **Tonkori** (plucked zither) — *Instrument.* The traditional 5-string instrument of Sakhalin Ainu, revived by contemporary musician OKI (Oki Kano). Represents the ongoing revival: something almost dead, now breathing again and reaching global audiences.

**Why these five work together:** Each artifact represents a *phase* in a single historical story. They build a complete arc from origin to near-extinction to revival. A student can literally narrate them in order as a five-act story.

**Student oral exam confidence:** The Yukie Chiri story is deeply human and memorable. The colonial suppression angle directly connects to the essential questions about how stories marginalize groups. Students can make a clear argument: "The Japanese colonial story that the Ainu were 'primitive' and needed to be 'protected' justified taking everything — their land, their language, their religion."

### PROPOSAL C: "More Than Cloth — Reading an Ainu Attush Robe"

**Narrative Angle:** Maximum depth on a single object. The attush robe is not just clothing — it is a text, a prayer, a map of Ainu identity. Five artifacts that together explain how to *read* one Ainu garment: what it's made of, what its patterns mean, how it was worn, who wore it, and what happens when you can no longer wear it.

**Story Arc:** The robe itself (attush) → how it's made (elm bark fiber processing) → what its patterns encode (geometric art as protective spiritual language) → what you wear with it (matanpushi headband and tamasay necklace as ensemble) → what happens when clothing is banned and then reclaimed (Meiji-era ban on traditional dress; modern revival weavers).

**Why these five work together:** Very coherent — one cultural domain, fully explored.

**Student oral exam confidence:** A student could become an expert on one beautiful object. Simple, manageable, thorough.

There's no real dramatic arc here — it's more "look at all the parts of this outfit." It also struggles to connect to the essential questions about marginalization and social change. A student could explain the attush perfectly and still miss the whole colonial suppression story.

## 1. CHOSEN NARRATIVE

**"The Thread That Was Almost Cut: How Ainu Culture Survived"**

The exhibit tells a five-act story of survival. For thousands of years, the Ainu people of northern Japan built a complete civilization rooted in their belief that all living things are inhabited by divine spirits (*kamuy*) who must be honored through ceremony, craftsmanship, prayer, and song. Then, in 1899, the Japanese Meiji government enacted a law that called the Ainu "former aborigines" — and set about eliminating their religion, their land, their language, and their identity.

The exhibit asks: *When a government tries to erase a people's story, what happens?*

The answer is this: the story finds other ways to survive. A spiritual ceremony encoded in a bear skull. An identity woven into bark-fiber cloth. A prayer practice outlawed but never forgotten. A dying language captured by a teenager before she could be stopped. A five-stringed instrument resurrected by a musician who plays it on stages around the world.

This is not a story about a dead culture. It is a story about what happens when a culture refuses to die.

## 2. FINAL 5 ARTIFACTS (IN ORDER)

---

### Artifact 1 — Bear Skull from Iyomante (the Bear Sending Ceremony)

- **Name:** Bear Skull / Iyomante Ceremony Object
- **Category:** Ceremonial object
- **Description:** The decorated skull of a brown bear, mounted on a sacred ritual fence (*nusa*) surrounded by ritual wooden offerings (*inau*), following the Iyomante ceremony. The Iyomante was the most elaborate ceremony in Ainu life: a bear cub was captured and raised by the community for 1–2 years (sometimes nursed by an Ainu woman), then ritually killed — not as slaughter, but as a sacred act of "sending the spirit home" to the divine world. The bear was understood to be a manifestation of Kim-un Kamuy, the Bear God, who had come to visit the human world. By treating the bear with love during its life and ceremonial respect at its death, the Ainu honored their contract with the divine and ensured the gods would send more gifts. The bear's skull was preserved and honored as the permanent physical record of that ceremony. The Meiji government banned Iyomante in the late 19th century as "barbaric."
- **Why chosen:** The bear skull is the single most powerful object for establishing what the Ainu believed and why colonialism's attack on their religion was so devastating. It immediately shows the depth and coherence of Ainu spiritual life — this is not "primitive superstition" but a complete theological and ecological framework.
- **Cultural significance:** The Iyomante ceremony encapsulates the central Ainu value of *reciprocity* — humans receive gifts from nature and must return respect through ceremony. The bear skull represents the relationship between the human and divine worlds.
- **Exhibit role in arc:** Opens the story by establishing the living spiritual world. This is what the Ainu were before colonialism arrived.
- **Sources:** Wikipedia — Iomante (S4); Wikipedia — Ainu religion (S3); Wikipedia — Ainu people (S1)

---

### Artifact 2 — Attush Robe (Elm Bark Fiber Ceremonial Garment)

- **Name:** Attush (Attusi) — Traditional Ainu Bark-Fiber Robe
- **Category:** Clothing / textile art
- **Description:** A full-length robe woven from the inner bark of the Japanese elm (ohyo) tree, decorated with intricate dark blue embroidered geometric patterns called *Ainu mon'yō* — spirals, interlocking curves, and flowing symmetrical designs. The patterns are not purely decorative: they are believed to protect the wearer from evil spirits and encode spiritual meaning. Women wove and embroidered the attush; the craftsmanship was a lifelong skill. When the Meiji government enacted the 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act, Ainu people were pressured and in many cases compelled to abandon traditional dress and adopt Japanese clothing — a deliberate erasure of visible cultural identity. The attush today is one of the most recognized symbols of Ainu identity, held in museum collections worldwide including the British Museum and Japan's National Ainu Museum (Upopoy). Modern Ainu craftswomen are actively reviving attush weaving.
- **Why chosen:** The attush is the most visually striking artifact in the exhibit and serves as a bridge between the spiritual (the geometric patterns are protective prayers) and the political (the colonial government targeted dress as a mechanism of assimilation). A student can point to this robe and say: "this is an identity the government tried to make invisible."
- **Cultural significance:** The attush represents Ainu identity made tangible — it encodes spiritual protection, demonstrates extraordinary craft skill, and marks its wearer as Ainu. Its suppression and revival mirror the larger story of Ainu cultural survival.
- **Exhibit role in arc:** Deepens the picture of Ainu material and spiritual life. Shows that cultural identity was encoded not just in ceremonies but in everyday objects — which is why the Meiji erasure effort targeted them.
- **Sources:** Wikipedia — Ainu culture (S2); Wikipedia — Ainu people (S1); British Museum collections

---

### Artifact 3 — Ikupasuy (Prayer Stick / Libation Stick)

- **Name:** Ikupasuy — Ainu Prayer Stick
- **Category:** Ceremonial tool
- **Description:** A flat, polished wooden stick approximately 20–30 cm long, carved with flowing geometric patterns (Ainu mon'yō). The ikupasuy was used by the head of an Ainu household to offer prayers and libations (sake or millet wine) to the *kamuy* — it was dipped into a cup of the offering liquid and used to gesture or "pass" the offering toward the divine being being addressed. The ikupasuy was the daily, intimate medium between humans and the spiritual world — used at every meal, every ceremony, every prayer. It was, in the most literal sense, the Ainu telephone to God. The 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act banned Ainu religious practice, which included the ceremonial use of objects like the ikupasuy. Families who continued praying with them did so in secret.
- **Why chosen:** The ikupasuy makes the colonial suppression story concrete and personal. It's not abstract — this is a specific object that a specific person picked up at every meal to speak to their god, and a specific law that said: *stop*. The carved geometric patterns also connect visually to the attush robe, showing how the same spiritual language runs through all Ainu objects.
- **Cultural significance:** The ikupasuy is the most intimate expression of Ainu spirituality — not a grand public ceremony but a daily household act. Its suppression represents the invasion of colonial authority into the most private spaces of Ainu life.
- **Exhibit role in arc:** The pivot point of the story. The exhibit shifts here from "here is what the Ainu believed" to "here is what was banned." The ikupasuy is the hinge between the living culture (Artifacts 1–2) and the near-extinction narrative (Artifacts 4–5).
- **Sources:** Wikipedia — Ainu religion (S3); Wikipedia — Ainu people (S1); Wikipedia — Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act (S13); Upopoy/National Ainu Museum catalog

---

### Artifact 4 — *Ainu Shin'yoshū* Manuscript (Yukie Chiri, 1922)

- **Name:** *Ainu Shin'yoshū* — Manuscript/Book by Yukie Chiri (1922)
- **Category:** Written / recorded material
- **Description:** A physical book published in 1922 by Yukie Chiri (1903–1922), a young Ainu woman who, working against time, transcribed and translated Ainu oral epic poetry (*kamuy yukar*) into Japanese. The Ainu had no indigenous writing system — their entire cultural knowledge, including cosmology, history, ethics, and spiritual beliefs, was transmitted through oral performance. By Chiri's era, that tradition was dying: the 1899 colonial law had banned Ainu language in schools, and each elder who died took irreplaceable stories with them. Chiri, born in 1903 in Hokkaido, began collecting and transcribing these epics as a teenager, assisted by linguist Bronisław Piłsudski's earlier recordings. She completed the manuscript and sent it to be typeset. She died of a heart condition at age 19, just months before it was published. The book she left behind became one of the most important records of Ainu oral literature in existence.
- **Why chosen:** Yukie Chiri's story is the emotional heart of the exhibit — a 19-year-old Ainu woman saving her culture with a pen while her government tried to erase it. It is also the most direct connection to the *yukar* oral tradition and to the language extinction crisis. The *Ainu Shin'yoshū* is a real, findable object with a real cover image and a documented history.
- **Cultural significance:** The manuscript represents the oral tradition — the entire Ainu knowledge system — captured at the last possible moment. Chiri's act is itself an act of resistance: she used the colonizer's tool (writing, Japanese language) to preserve the colonized culture's knowledge.
- **Exhibit role in arc:** The crisis point. By this artifact, the student has established what the culture was (Artifacts 1–3) and now confronts how close it came to total extinction — represented by a teenager working against her own death and her culture's erasure simultaneously.
- **Sources:** Wikipedia — Ainu people (S1); Wikipedia — Yukar (S5); Wikipedia — Ainu language (S10); Chiri Yukie biographical sources

---

### Artifact 5 — Tonkori (Plucked Zither) and Its Contemporary Revival

- **Name:** Tonkori — Traditional Ainu Plucked Zither
- **Category:** Musical instrument
- **Description:** A long, narrow plucked string instrument of the Sakhalin Ainu, traditionally strung with five strings (sometimes three or six), played by plucking with both hands. The name "tonkori" is onomatopoeic — the sound it makes. Like all Ainu musical instruments, the tonkori is considered to be imbued with its own spirit (*ramat*). The instrument nearly disappeared during the 20th century as Sakhalin Ainu communities were displaced and their culture suppressed. It was revived in the late 20th and early 21st centuries by **OKI (Oki Kano)**, an Ainu-Japanese musician born to an Ainu father and Japanese mother, who studied the instrument and began performing globally — blending traditional Ainu tonkori technique with dub, reggae, and world music. OKI's albums have been released internationally; he has performed across Europe, North America, and Asia, bringing Ainu music to audiences who had never heard of the Ainu people. His music has become one of the most visible forms of Ainu cultural advocacy.
- **Why chosen:** The tonkori provides the story's resolution — not a triumphant "problem solved" ending, but a genuine, living, ongoing act of cultural reclamation. OKI's story (Ainu father, Japanese mother, playing the tonkori at international festivals) mirrors the reality of modern Ainu identity: mixed, proud, and refusing to be erased. A student can *listen* to this artifact via YouTube, which is unique among the five and deeply engaging.
- **Cultural significance:** The tonkori represents the survival and revival of Ainu musical tradition, and music's role as both prayer and cultural memory. OKI embodies the complexity of contemporary Ainu identity — not a museum piece but a living presence.
- **Exhibit role in arc:** The resolution. The exhibit ends not with extinction or tragedy but with a living Ainu musician on a world stage, playing an instrument that was nearly lost. The story is not over.
- **Sources:** Wikipedia — Tonkori (S7); Wikipedia — Ainu folk music (S6); Wikipedia — Ainu people (S1); OKI music catalog and press materials

## 4. ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS CONNECTION

The assignment's three essential questions (EQs) are: (1) how do stories shape identity? (2) how do stories privilege or marginalize groups? (3) how do stories drive social change?

**EQ1 — How Stories Shape Identity:**
The bear skull and the ikupasuy answer this directly. The Ainu *kamuy* narrative — the story that all living things are divine and must be honored — shapes every aspect of Ainu identity: how they hunt (with prayer), how they dress (with protective patterns), how they pray (with the ikupasuy), how they raise their children (with yukar oral epics). Identity is not a separate thing from the story; the story *is* the identity. When the colonial government banned the story, it was explicitly trying to destroy the identity.

**EQ2 — How Stories Privilege or Marginalize Groups:**
The ikupasuy and Chiri's manuscript answer this. The Japanese colonial story — encoded in the 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act — called the Ainu "former aborigines" who needed "protection" from their own backwardness. This story justified taking their land, banning their language, destroying their religious practices, and forcing their children into schools where only Japanese was spoken. The word "former" was deliberate: it told a story in which the Ainu had already ceased to exist as a distinct people. That narrative was used to marginalize a population of ~80,000 people across three centuries into near-invisibility. The exhibit's intro board should name this explicitly.

**EQ3 — How Stories Drive Social Change:**
Both Chiri's manuscript and the tonkori answer this. Chiri's decision to *write down* the yukar — to translate the Ainu oral tradition into Japanese, using the colonizer's language and tools — was a political act as much as a scholarly one. It said: *our stories exist, they have value, and they will outlast your erasure.* OKI's music takes that act further: the tonkori is playing in concert halls in Berlin and New York, telling the world that the Ainu exist. The 2019 Ainu Policy Promotion Act — Japan's first legal recognition of Ainu as indigenous people — came partly because Ainu people like Shigeru Kayano, Yukie Chiri, and OKI told their story loudly enough that the world listened. Stories drove that change.

## 5. INTRO BOARD GUIDANCE

The introduction board (500–600 words) must cover the following points, in this order:

1. **Who the Ainu are:** The indigenous people of Hokkaido, Sakhalin, and the Kuril Islands — Japan's northernmost territories. Their homeland is called *Ainu Mosir* ("land of the humans"). Their population was approximately 80,000 in the 18th century; today, official counts show around 11,450 — though actual numbers of people with Ainu ancestry may be ten to twenty times higher due to assimilation.

2. **Their worldview — the kamuy:** Everything in the natural world — bears, salmon, fire, wind, the sea — is inhabited by a divine spirit called a *kamuy*. The Ainu understood themselves to be in a sacred reciprocal relationship with these spirits: nature provides life; humans provide respect, ceremony, and gratitude in return. This is not metaphor. This is law.

3. **What colonialism did:** In 1899, the Japanese Meiji government passed the Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act. The law confiscated Ainu land, banned Ainu language from schools, prohibited traditional religious ceremonies, and forced Ainu people into a Japanese agricultural lifestyle they were not equipped or willing to adopt. The law called them "former aborigines" — as if they had already ceased to exist as a distinct people. It remained in force for nearly a century.

4. **What was almost lost:** The Ainu language is now critically endangered — possibly with no fully fluent native speakers remaining as of 2025. The oral tradition (the *yukar* epic poetry that transmitted all Ainu knowledge for millennia) can no longer be performed in its original form. Sacred ceremonies were banned for generations. The loss is not abstract: it is the near-erasure of a civilization.

5. **What this exhibit is about:** This exhibit tells the story through five objects — each one a thread in the fabric of Ainu culture. Together, they answer one question: *when a government tries to cut the thread of an indigenous people's story, what happens?* The answer these five objects give is not comforting, and it is not simple. But it is true: the thread was almost cut. It was not.

6. **The present:** In 2019, Japan officially recognized the Ainu as indigenous people for the first time in national law. In 2020, the National Ainu Museum (Upopoy) opened in Hokkaido. Ainu musicians perform internationally. Craftswomen are reviving bark-fiber weaving. Young people are learning the language from recordings. The story is not over.

**Tone guidance for the intro board:** Do not romanticize or treat the Ainu as a "disappeared" people. Do not sanitize the colonial history. Write with respect and with the understanding that the Ainu are a living community with living grievances and living hopes. The intro should be engaging enough that a high school student reads it fully before moving to the artifacts — aim for narrative, not encyclopedia entry.
