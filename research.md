## 1. HISTORY & TIMELINE

### Origins
- The Ainu are descended from the **Jōmon people**, the indigenous hunter-gatherers of Japan who lived across the archipelago roughly 14,000–300 BCE. [S1]
- Their immediate cultural antecedents are linked to the **Satsumon culture** (Epi-Jōmon period, from ~5th c. BCE) and later the **Okhotsk culture** — both of which merged into a recognizable "Ainu cultural complex" beginning around the **13th century CE**. [S1]
- Three main cultural layers: (1) deep Jōmon heritage in subsistence, worldview, and language; (2) Japanese cultural influence through centuries of contact; (3) northern "Okhotsk" influence from Siberian neighbors. [S1]
- Genetically, the Ainu are closely related to East Asian populations, rooted in the ancient Jōmon lineage. [S1]

### Historical Ethnonym
- "Ainu" means *a human being* in the Ainu language — specifically contrasted with *kamuy* (divine beings). [S1]
- Historical Japanese texts called them **Emishi** (蝦夷), meaning "barbarians" or "hairy people" — considered derogatory today. [S1]
- The Qing dynasty called Sakhalin **Kuyedao** ("Island of the Ainu"). [S1]

### The 1899 Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act — Details
- Enacted by the Imperial Diet during the Meiji era.
- Officially framed as "protection," but in practice confiscated Ainu lands and mandated assimilation.
- Forced Ainu into agriculture on poor-quality land grants; prohibited traditional hunting and fishing.
- Banned the Ainu language in schools and the practice of traditional animistic religion.
- Placed Ainu financial resources under government control for "welfare" purposes.
- Ainu children received only rudimentary education in Japanese focused on "national ethics."
- Survived nearly a century; repealed in 1997. [S13, S14]

### Colonization Effects
- Population crash: ~80,000 Ainu in 18th century → ~17,000 by the 1880s (Hokkaido) [S14]
- Ainu lands distributed to Yamato Japanese settlers for Western-style industrial farming.
- Infrastructure (roads, railways, factories) built using Ainu and Korean forced labor. [S1]
- During Meiji, animal sacrifice (Iyomante) and tattooing were ordered to cease. [S1]
- Ainu women frequently raped or forcibly married to Japanese men; men deported as indentured labor. [S1]

### The 2019 Recognition Law (Ainu Policy Promotion Act)
- Formally designates the Ainu as "an indigenous people of Japan" — the first time this has been stated in national law.
- Created legal framework for promoting Ainu culture, language, and traditions.
- Established the concept of *symbolic spaces for ethnic harmony* — leading to the Upopoy complex.
- Included provisions for the repatriation and respectful storage of Ainu human remains. [S1, S11]
- **Criticism**: Many Ainu activists noted the law does not include land rights, resource rights, or political self-determination. [S10]

## 2. GEOGRAPHY & TRADITIONAL TERRITORIES

### Traditional Homeland: "Ainu Mosir"
- The Ainu called their traditional territory **Ainu Mosir** (アイヌモシㇼ) — "the land of the Ainu" or "the land of humans." [S1]
- This vast territory historically included:
  - **Hokkaido** (main island; cultural heartland)
  - **Southern half of Sakhalin** (called Karafuto in Japanese)
  - **Kuril Islands** (chain stretching northeast of Hokkaido toward Kamchatka)
  - **Kamchatka Peninsula** (southeastern tip)
  - **Khabarovsk Krai** (coastal areas of mainland Russia)
  - Northern parts of **Honshu** (Tohoku region; evidenced by Ainu place-names)
- The Ainu had occupied these areas since before the arrival of the Yamato Japanese and Russians. [S1]

### Current Population Centers (Post-Colonization)
- Most Ainu today live in **Hokkaido**, concentrated especially in:
  - Southeastern Hidaka region (towns like **Nibutani/Niputay** in Biratori)
  - **Sapporo** (Hokkaido's capital)
  - **Shiraoi** (site of the new National Museum/Upopoy)
  - **Asahikawa** and other interior towns
- Small Ainu communities in **Kanto** (Tokyo area), where some migrated for work. [S10]
- **Russia**: ~300 Ainu counted in 2021 census; located in Sakhalin Oblast, Kamchatka Krai, and Khabarovsk Krai. [S1]

### Population Numbers
- Official 2023 Hokkaido survey: **11,450 respondents** identified as Ainu.
- Unofficial estimates (2002): **200,000 or more** people of Ainu descent in Japan — the discrepancy reflects near-total assimilation and many individuals having no knowledge of their ancestry. [S1]
- Historical high: ~80,000 Ainu in the 18th century. By 1868, ~15,000 in Hokkaido. [S1]

### Place Names
- Much of Hokkaido's toponymy comes from Ainu — e.g., "Sapporo" from Ainu "sat poro pet" (dry great river); "Hokkaido" itself is a Japanization of "Ainu Mosir."
- Ainu place-names throughout Tohoku support historical Ainu presence there. [S1]

## 3. SPIRITUAL BELIEFS & COSMOLOGY

### The Kamuy (Divine Spirits)
- **Central concept**: The Ainu worldview is deeply **animistic**. All things in the natural world — animals, plants, mountains, rivers, fire, wind, rain, thunder — are inhabited by **kamuy** (divine spirits). [S1, S3]
- "Kamuy" (カムイ) literally contrasts with "ainu" (human being) — the two categories define the cosmos.
- Kamuy are not abstract deities but *active presences* in the world. They can be benevolent or dangerous.
- Humans and kamuy are in a **reciprocal relationship**: humans show respect and offer prayers (kamuy nomi); kamuy provide animals, fish, food, and protection in return. [S3]
- Animals are understood as kamuy who have *put on* bodies of flesh and come to visit the human world, bringing gifts (meat, fur). When treated with ritual respect, their spirits return to the divine world happy and may return again. [S4]

### The Ramat (Spiritual Essence)
- Every being — human or kamuy — possesses a **ramat**: a soul/spiritual essence.
- This essence can leave the body; illness may be caused by the ramat being disturbed. [S3]
- The concept extends to objects: even tools and instruments are considered to have spirits. [S6]

### Key Kamuy (Divine Beings)
- **Kamuy-huci (Fuchi)** — Goddess of the hearth fire; the most important and daily-present deity. She is the intermediary between humans and the rest of the kamuy world — prayers are offered through her. [S1, S3]
- **Kim-un Kamuy** — "Master of the Mountains" (the brown bear god); one of the most powerful and revered kamuy. [S4]
- **Repun Kamuy** — God of the sea / the orca; worshipped by coastal Ainu. [S3]
- **Kanto-kor-kamuy** — Sky deity/thunder god. [S3]
- **Kotan-kor-kamuy** — Blakiston's fish owl; "master of the village." [S3]
- **Mosiri-kor-kamuy** — God of earthquakes. [S3]
- Salmon is called **kamuy chep** ("god's fish") or **shipe** ("true food") — deeply sacred. [S1]

### Cosmology
- The universe is divided into realms: the world of humans (ainu mosir), the divine/upper world (kamuy mosir), and the underworld.
- The **hearth fire** is the axis connecting these worlds; the fire goddess mediates all communication. [S3]
- The **eastern window** of a cise (house), called *rorun-puyar*, is sacred: gods enter and leave through it; ceremonial tools are passed through it. Humans are forbidden to look in through it. [S1]

### Ceremonies and Prayer (Kamuy Nomi)
- **Nomi / Kamuy nomi**: prayer/ceremony. Often involves the offering of *ikupasuy* (prayer stick) dipped in sake, and *inau* (carved ritual offerings). [S3]
- **Iyomante** (see below in Cultural Practices) — the bear ceremony.
- **Cipor-nomi**: a blessing ceremony for a new boat.
- The *nusa* (ritual fence of inau) is placed outdoors at shrines. [S3]
- **Icharpa**: ceremony to appease the spirits of the dead; important funerary traditions. [S3]
- Offerings typically include *tonoto* (millet sake), dried fish, inau, and ikupasuy. [S1, S3]

## 4. CULTURAL PRACTICES

### Oral Tradition — Yukar and Other Epics
- Because the Ainu had **no indigenous writing system**, their entire cultural knowledge — history, religion, law, ethics, cosmology — was transmitted orally through epic poetry and narrative. [S5, S6]
- **Yukar** (ユカラ): the central epic oral tradition. Long, rhythmic, sung/chanted performances from memory — sometimes lasting many hours or even days at communal gatherings. [S1, S5]
  - Two main types:
    1. **Kamuy yukar** — epics featuring kamuy (divine beings); told in **first person from the god's perspective** — a distinctive Ainu literary feature. Example: the bear god recounts being killed and his soul being sent home. [S5, S6]
    2. **Ainu yukar** (hero epics) — stories of human culture heroes. [S5]
  - Sung unaccompanied; singer may tap *repni* (wooden blocks) on the floor or hearth as percussion. [S6]
  - Classical Ainu of the yukar is highly polysynthetic — more complex than everyday speech. [S10]
- **Uepeker / Uwepeker**: prose narratives / folktales; shorter, less formal. [S1, S5]
- **Upopo**: group songs — communal, often rhythmic and accompanied by the mukkuri or tonkori. [S6]
- **Rekukhara**: throat singing tradition from the Sakhalin Ainu; performed in pairs; comparable to Inuit katajjaq. [S6]
- **Yaisama**: improvised singing/expression of emotions; comparable to freestyle. [S6]
- Notable transcriber: **Yukie Chiri** (1903–1922), a young Ainu woman who transcribed and translated Ainu epic tales into Japanese before her death at age 19. Her work is a key source for modern study. [S1]

### Music and Dance
- Music is sacred: "almost every type of Ainu song is sacred, and even musical instruments are said to be imbued with souls." [S6]
- **Rimse**: ceremonial round/group dance; performed at important ceremonies. [S6]
- Songs function as prayer in many contexts — before meals, after fishing, during hunting preparations. [S6]
- **Upopo**: group chants/songs, often performed by women while doing domestic work; not merely labor songs but magic-oriented (to ward off evil, attract good). [S6]
- **Lullabies** (Iyon'nokka in Hidaka; Ihunke in Asahikawa): mothers sing with tongue-rolling sounds. [S6]

### Tattooing
- **Traditional Ainu tattoo practice**: Central to female identity and adulthood.
- Women tattooed **around the mouth** (creating a "mustache" shape) and on the **forearms** and backs of hands; men could have facial tattoos as well (cheeks, forehead, eyebrows). [S1]
- Girls began tattooing as young as age 5, typically more fully between ages **10–13**; tattoos expanded over time until the woman was ready to marry (~15–16). [S1]
- Ink: **soot deposited on a pot hung over a birch-bark fire**. [S1]
- The Meiji government ordered Ainu to cease tattooing as part of the forced assimilation policy. [S1]

### The Bear Ceremony — Iyomante (Iomante)
- **Iyomante** (イヨマンテ) literally means "to send off" or "to send home."
- The most elaborate and famous Ainu ceremony; connected to the belief that the brown bear is a manifestation of **Kim-un Kamuy** (the divine bear spirit) visiting the human world. [S4]
- **Ritual process**: A bear cub is captured in spring and raised by the community — sometimes nursed by an Ainu woman — for 1–2 years. The cub is treated with great affection and respect as a divine guest. [S4]
- At the appointed ceremony, the bear is **ritually killed** (by strangulation with poles — a method that avoids shedding blood). This is understood not as killing but as *sending the spirit home* to the divine world, where the kamuy will report the kindness of its Ainu hosts and send more gifts (animals, fish) in return. [S4]
- The ceremony involves: elaborate prayers, singing (including the "Song of a Bear" kamuy yukar), feasting, offerings of inau and sake, dancing (rimse). [S4, S6]
- The bear's skull is preserved and placed on a nusa (ritual fence) decorated with inau. [S4]
- **Legal status**: The ceremony was banned by Japanese colonial authorities during the Meiji era as "barbaric." It remains controversial even today. It is now performed much less frequently and has come under animal rights criticism. [S4]
- UNESCO has expressed interest in preserving Iyomante as intangible cultural heritage. [S4]

### Marriage and Social Structure
- Society organized around **kotan** (villages), typically 5–10 households, located near rivers (salmon) or seashores. [S1]
- Led by a headman; community judgment used for disputes; no capital punishment — beating was the primary penalty; murderers had noses/ears cut off or tendons severed. [S1]
- Marriage: arranged at childhood; typical age ~15–16 for women, ~17–18 for men. Consensual marriages also existed. [S1]

### Hunting and Fishing
- Traditional Ainu subsistence was primarily **hunting and fishing**, later supplemented by gathering and limited agriculture.
- Key animals: brown bear, sika deer, salmon, trout, harbor seal, sea eagle (feathers for trade).
- Hunted with **poison-tipped arrows and spears**; poison (surku) derived from aconite roots — recipe was a household secret. [S1]
- Spring bear hunting: bears caught just after hibernation when they are weak. Summer: spring-loaded arrow traps (amappo). [S1]
- **Iwor**: each village's hunting territory; trespassing harshly penalized. [S1]
- **Salmon** was the most important food source — caught in massive autumn runs, dried and preserved. Salmon called *kamuy chep* ("god's fish"). [S1]

## 5. MATERIAL CULTURE & ARTIFACTS

### Clothing
- **Attush / Attusi**: Traditional Ainu robe woven from **elm (ohyo) bark fiber** (or sometimes nettle or other plant fibers). Decorated with elaborate geometric embroidered patterns in dark blue/black on natural off-white. Patterns are distinctive and regional. [S1, S2]
- Attush robes feature a simple cut: straight sleeves, folded wrap-around, tied at the waist; hem to the calves. [S1]
- Women wore an undergarment of Japanese cloth (obtained through trade). [S1]
- **Winter clothing**: Animal skins; deerskin leggings; in Sakhalin, boots made from dog or salmon skin. [S1]
- **Matanpushi**: embroidered headbands worn by women (and historically men). [S1]
- **Ninkari**: metal hoop earrings with spherical pendants; traditional women's jewelry (historically gender-neutral). [S1]
- **Sapanpe**: ceremonial crown for men, made of woven wood fiber with wooden animal-god figures. Worn for important ceremonies. [S1]
- **Emush**: ceremonial sword (not a weapon) worn by men on a shoulder strap (emush-at) during ceremonies. [S1]
- **Tamasay / Shitoki**: beaded necklace of glass balls worn by women; glass sourced from trade with mainland Asia. [S1]

### Tools and Knives
- **Makiri**: small general-purpose knife used for carving, food preparation, and daily tasks. Often decorated with carved designs.
- **Menoko-makiri**: a version specifically associated with women.
- **Tashiro** (or various whittling knives): used for carving inau and other wooden objects.
- Traditional Ainu are skilled woodcarvers; geometric spiral and flowing patterns characterize Ainu art on wood, bark, and cloth.

### Musical Instruments
- **Tonkori** (トンコリ): A **plucked zither** instrument, traditionally of the **Sakhalin Ainu**. Usually has 5 strings (sometimes 3 or 6). Played by plucking; produces a melodic, resonant sound. The name is onomatopoeic. Contemporary musician **OKI (Oki Kano)** — the son of an Ainu father and Japanese mother — has revived the tonkori and is the most famous current performer of Ainu music. [S6, S7]
- **Mukkuri** (ムックリ): A **jaw harp (mouth harp)** made from bamboo. Played primarily by women. Sound produced by manipulating a string attached to the bamboo reed; the mouth acts as a resonance box, allowing tone variation. Historically considered somewhat informal ("children's toy") but popular today as a tourist souvenir and cultural item. [S6]
- **Repni**: simple wooden tapping sticks used to keep rhythm during yukar recitation. [S6]
- Additional instruments documented but no longer active: various flutes and percussion. [S6]

### Ceremonial Objects
- **Inau** (イナウ): **Ritual wooden shavings** — the most ubiquitous Ainu sacred object. Made by shaving a stick of willow, white birch, or certain other woods so that the curling shavings remain attached to the stick, creating a distinctive "feathered" or "curled" appearance. Used as offerings to kamuy at all ceremonies. Placed at the *nusa* (outdoor ritual fence), passed through the sacred eastern window, offered at hearths, graves, and water sources. Different styles of inau are made for different kamuy. [S8]
- **Ikupasuy** (イクパスイ): Prayer stick (also called *tuki pasuy*). A flat wooden stick, finely carved with Ainu geometric patterns. Used by the head of the household to offer sake or other libations to the kamuy — the ikupasuy is dipped into the offering cup and used to gesture toward the kamuy being addressed. Considered a medium between human and divine. [S1, S3]
- **Nusa**: Outdoor sacred fence or altar where clusters of inau are placed; connects the human settlement to the divine world. [S3]
- **Sintoko / Hokai**: Lacquerware containers used to store valuables and sacred items. Placed on the *iyoykir* platform inside the house. [S1]
- **Ikayop**: Quiver for arrows; kept with valuables on the iyoykir platform. [S1]

### Food and Cooking Culture
- **Salmon** (kamuy chep): Most important food. Autumn salmon runs provided massive quantities dried for winter preservation. Also a major trade item with the Japanese. [S1]
- **Iyuta**: fish cake / preserved fish preparations.
- **Tonoto**: millet sake brewed for ceremonial use (used as offering to kamuy). Made from millet (piyapa), foxtail millet (munchiro), or barnyard millet (menkur). [S1]
- Bear, deer, fox, wolf, rabbit, birds — always **cooked** (boiled or roasted); raw meat was taboo. [S1]
- **Turep**: bulbs of *Cardiocrinum cordatum* — starch extracted and preserved; foundation of Ainu starch cuisine. Also made potatoes easy to adopt when they arrived. [S1]
- **Lacquerware**: Imported from Honshu, used as prestige tableware and ceremonial vessels; treated as treasure. [S1]

### Art and Carving
- Ainu art is characterized by flowing, scrolling **geometric patterns** (called Ainu mon'yō) — spirals, interlocking curves — applied to robes, carvings, and household objects.
- Wood carving is practiced by both men and women; tools include makiri and other knives.
- Traditional objects beautifully decorated include prayer sticks (ikupasuy), bear skulls (for ceremony), earrings, necklaces, and house posts.
- Ainu mon'yō patterns have been designated part of **Hokkaido Heritage**. [S1]

## 6. CORE VALUES

### 1. Reciprocity with the Natural World (Kamuy-nomi)
- The central ethical principle of Ainu life: **all living things are in a relationship of mutual obligation**. Humans receive gifts (food, resources) from nature; they must return respect, gratitude, and proper ceremony.
- Failing to honor a kamuy — through disrespect, waste, or improper ritual — results in misfortune, illness, or the withdrawal of the kamuy's gifts.
- This ethic shapes every aspect of life: hunting involves prayer beforehand and thanks afterward; salmon are never wasted; bears are killed only with ceremony.
- The Iyomante ceremony is the ultimate expression of this value — the "gift" of the bear's life is returned to the divine world with elaborate gratitude. [S1, S3, S4]

### 2. Community and the Kotan
- The *kotan* (village) is the fundamental unit of Ainu social life. Decisions are communal; individuals depend on the group for survival and judgment. [S1]
- Hospitality (*irenka*) is a core virtue; community members help one another with food and labor.
- Women held significant social roles — as hearers and transmitters of oral tradition, as weavers, and as the ones who raise bear cubs for Iyomante. [S6]

### 3. Oral Transmission of Knowledge
- With no indigenous writing, the Ainu developed extraordinary traditions of **memorization and oral transmission**.
- Elders were the repositories of cultural knowledge; the yukar tradition ensured this knowledge was preserved through performance.
- The loss of native speakers and elders in the 20th century represented a catastrophic knowledge extinction event — hence the urgency of revitalization. [S1, S5, S6]

### 4. Respect for the Dead and Ancestors
- Proper funerary rites are essential; the *icharpa* ceremony appeases the souls of the dead.
- Disturbing Ainu graves — as Japanese researchers did in the Meiji era — is a profound violation of Ainu spiritual ethics, which is why the repatriation issue carries such emotional weight. [S3]

### 5. Non-Violence and Balance
- Capital punishment did not exist in traditional Ainu law; disputes were resolved through community judgment and proportional physical punishment. [S1]
- Hunting was bounded by territory, ritual, and the rule of reciprocity — not unlimited extraction. [S1]

## 7. REPATRIATION OF REMAINS & ARTIFACTS

### Background: Ainu Graves Robbed in the Meiji Era
- During the Meiji period (late 19th – early 20th c.), **Japanese researchers and colonial officials exhumed Ainu graves** to collect skeletal remains for "scientific" (racial anthropological) study. This practice was widespread and largely unregulated.
- The University of Hokkaido (Hokkaido Imperial University) accumulated approximately **1,636 Ainu skeletal remains** — the largest collection in Japan. [S1]
- Other Japanese universities (Sapporo Medical University, Kyoto University, etc.) also held remains.
- Western institutions also collected Ainu remains and cultural objects during this period:
  - **University of Edinburgh** (Scotland)
  - **University of Aberdeen** (Scotland)
  - **University of Cambridge**
  - **University of Vienna**
  - Natural history museums in the UK and Europe [S1]

### The Hokkaido University Controversy
- Hokkaido University held ~1,636 sets of Ainu remains — collected from more than 20 locations across Hokkaido, largely without consent.
- **2012**: The University of Aberdeen (Scotland) returned **two Ainu skulls** to the Ainu Association of Hokkaido — the **first international repatriation** of Ainu remains. [S1]
- Multiple universities, confronted with this history, began returning remains to Ainu communities and organizations.
- By the time the 2019 law was enacted, **12 Japanese universities** had agreed to return remains to a centralized national memorial.

### The 2019 Law and Repatriation Framework
- The **Ainu Policy Promotion Act (2019)** established the Upopoy complex (National Ainu Museum + National Ainu Park) in Shiraoi, which includes a **memorial facility (慰霊施設)** on the east side of Lake Poroto — a sacred site where Ainu services are held for the remains of ancestors. [S11]
- Remains returned by universities are to be housed in this memorial and eventually returned to the communities from which they originated.
- The memorial was completed in 2019; the museum opened 2020.
- **Criticism**: Many Ainu activists have criticized the centralized repository model as still removing remains from the specific communities of origin. Demands for direct return of remains to local communities, rather than to a government-controlled site, continue. [S10]

### Which Museums Hold Ainu Artifacts?
- **British Museum** (London): holds Ainu ceremonial dress and artifacts, including a ceremonial attush robe. [S1]
- **Smithsonian Institution** (Washington, D.C.): holds ethnographic Ainu collections.
- **Nibutani Ainu Culture Museum** (Hokkaido): founded by Shigeru Kayano, holds his personal collection of hundreds of Ainu artifacts. [S9]
- **Hakodate City Museum of Northern Peoples** (Hokkaido): extensive Ainu collection. [S11]
- **Historical Museum of the Saru River** (Biratori, Hokkaido): focused on the Hidaka region Ainu. [S11]
- **National Ainu Museum / Upopoy** (Shiraoi, Hokkaido): Japan's premier national institution for Ainu culture. [S11]
- Various other natural history museums in Europe and the United States.

### Repatriation of Cultural Objects
- The repatriation conversation covers not only human remains but also sacred objects (inau, ikupasuy, ceremonial garments) held in foreign museums.
- Progress on artifact repatriation has been slower than on remains.
- The 2019 law does not specifically compel international repatriation of cultural artifacts; this remains an ongoing Ainu advocacy issue.

## 8. MODERN AINU — REVITALIZATION & RECOGNITION

### The Ainu Language Today
- The Hokkaido Ainu language is **critically endangered / dormant**: as of the 2010s, no known native speakers remain; the last two native speakers were elderly and died around 2008–2021. [S10]
- As of 2025, some researchers report 2 possible native semi-speakers; a growing number of **neo-speakers** (people learning Ainu as a second language). [S10]
- 2023 Hokkaido survey: only 0.8% of respondents can hold a conversation in Ainu; 8.9% can speak a little. [S10]
- Sakhalin Ainu and Kuril Ainu languages are **extinct**. [S1]
- Ainu has no indigenous writing system; modern transcription uses **katakana** or the **Latin alphabet**. [S10]

### Language Revitalization Efforts
- **Shigeru Kayano** opened the first Ainu language school in 1987, funded by the Ainu Kyokai (Ainu Association). He went on to found 15 Ainu language schools. [S1, S9]
- **Agency for Cultural Affairs** (Japan) archives Ainu speech recordings.
- **YouTube educational channels** teaching conversational Ainu have been created. [S10]
- Bus announcements in Hokkaido can now be heard in Ainu on some routes. [S10]
- The National Ainu Museum states that "one of our main objectives is to preserve and revive the language." [S10]
- Ainu oral literature has been documented both for safeguarding and as teaching material. [S1]
- In 2019, Ainu was officially recognized as an indigenous language by the Japanese government. [S10]

### Notable Ainu People
- **Shigeru Kayano (萱野茂)** (1926–2006): Ainu activist, politician, author, and linguist. Born in Nibutani (Biratori), Hokkaido. One of the last native Ainu speakers. Founded the Nibutani Ainu Culture Museum (1972). Led successful effort to repeal the 1899 Protection Act. First Ainu Diet member (House of Councillors, 1994–1998), where he sometimes posed questions in the Ainu language. Published ~100 books on Ainu culture and language, including 28 yukar collections. Led protest against the Nibutani Dam, which flooded sacred Ainu land. [S9]
- **Yukie Chiri (知里幸恵)** (1903–1922): Ainu woman who, despite dying at age 19, transcribed and translated Ainu oral epics into Japanese — a foundational act of linguistic and cultural preservation. Her relative Imekanu was also an important cultural figure. [S1]
- **OKI (Oki Kano)**: Contemporary Ainu musician born to an Ainu father and Japanese mother. Has internationally revived the tonkori zither and blends Ainu musical traditions with contemporary genres. [S1, S6]
- **Abe Shinzo** (note: *not* Ainu, but relevant): His deputy PM Asō Tarō's 2020 claim that Japan has "one language, one ethnic group" was widely criticized by Ainu advocates as erasing Ainu existence. [S1]

### The 2020 Upopoy Complex — National Ainu Museum & Park
- **Location**: Shiraoi, Hokkaido (about 1 hour by car from Sapporo; 40 min from New Chitose Airport).
- **Official name**: National Ainu Museum (国立アイヌ民族博物館, Kokuritsu Ainu Minzoku Hakubutsukan).
- Part of **Upopoy** (ウポポイ) — an Ainu word meaning "singing together in a large group." The Upopoy complex comprises three facilities:
  1. The National Ainu Museum
  2. National Ainu Park (with reconstructed traditional kotan/village by Lake Poroto)
  3. Memorial facility (on high ground, east side of Lake Poroto — where Ainu services are held for repatriated remains) [S11]
- **Opening**: Officially opened July 12, 2020, after COVID-19 delayed the original April 24, 2020 date. [S11]
- **Mission**: "To promote a proper understanding and awareness of Ainu history and culture in Japan and elsewhere out of respect for the dignity of the indigenous Ainu people, while contributing to the creation and development of new aspects of Ainu culture." [S11]
- **Six exhibit themes**:
  1. Language
  2. History
  3. Views of the world (cosmology/religion)
  4. Livelihoods (hunting, fishing, farming, gathering)
  5. Lifestyles (food, clothing, shelters, music, dance)
  6. Trade and exchange with surrounding peoples [S11]
- The museum supersedes and replaces the former Ainu Museum (Porotokotan), which closed March 31, 2018. [S11]
- The **traditional Ainu village (kotan)** with reconstructed cise (houses) remains on the grounds for visitors to experience. [S11]
- **Criticism**: Some Ainu activists note the museum fails to acknowledge the history of Japanese discrimination against the Ainu, and the government has refused to apologize for past wrongs. [S10]
- **International reach**: As of early 2026, the museum's collections were touring internationally — a special exhibition titled "aynu" was on display at the Museu Etnològic i de Cultures del Món in Barcelona (December 2025 – June 2026). [Museum website, accessed May 2026]

### Other Cultural Institutions
- **Nibutani Ainu Culture Museum** (Biratori, Hokkaido): Founded by Shigeru Kayano; holds hundreds of his personally collected artifacts. [S9]
- **Hakodate City Museum of Northern Peoples**: Northern Hokkaido. [S11]
- **Historical Museum of the Saru River** (Biratori): Focused on Hidaka-region Ainu. [S11]
- **Pirka Kotan** (Sapporo/Jozankei area): Ainu language and cultural center. [S10]

### Cultural Revitalization Activities
- Traditional craft weaving of attush robes has experienced revival — modern Ainu craftswomen's work commands high prices. [S1]
- Ainu dance (rimse) and music groups perform at events and festivals.
- The **Ainu Kyokai (Ainu Association of Hokkaido)** continues advocacy work.
- Food: A small number of restaurants in Tokyo and Hokkaido now serve traditional Ainu cuisine. [S1]
- International Ainu music: OKI and others bring Ainu sounds to global audiences.
- Academic study: Hokkaido University's Center for Ainu and Indigenous Studies was established partially in response to the university's own remains controversy.

### Ongoing Challenges and Advocacy Issues
- **No land or resource rights**: The 2019 law recognizes Ainu identity but does not grant land rights, fishing rights, or resource rights — a major gap compared to indigenous peoples' rights frameworks internationally. [S14]
- **No political self-determination**: No constitutional or legislative mechanism for Ainu representation or self-governance. [S14]
- **Repatriation incomplete**: Remains held by international institutions not yet returned.
- **Language near-extinction**: Despite revitalization efforts, intergenerational transmission has not been re-established.
- **Continued discrimination**: Economic and social disparities persist; Ainu households still below average Japanese income levels. [S1]
- **Ethnic erasure rhetoric**: Mainstream Japanese political discourse still often frames Japan as ethnically homogenous, marginalizing Ainu identity. [S1]
