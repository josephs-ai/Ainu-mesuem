FOREST INTO PRAYER
How the Ainu Carved the Forest into Spiraling Messages for Their Gods
Indigenous Cultures Research Project — Spring 2026
===================================================

INTRODUCTION BOARD
---------------------------------------------------

Look at the five objects in this room — a ceremonial robe, a prayer stick, a knife, a headband, and a flat sake-dipping wand, gathered from different hands across different decades for entirely different purposes. But if you look closely, really look, you'll find the same shape on every single one. It is a line that coils back on itself like a wave frozen mid-break, a spiral curling inward and outward at once.

That shape has a name: moreu. And it is the most important thing in this room.

The Ainu are the Indigenous people of Hokkaido, the large northern island of Japan. For thousands of years, they lived in deep forest — they hunted bear and deer, fished rivers thick with salmon, and learned to read the land the way others read books. They didn't divide the world into sacred and ordinary. To the Ainu, everything alive carried a spirit, a kamuy: a fire burning in a hearth, a bear moving through the forest, an owl overhead. The forest wasn't scenery. It was congregation.

Among the oldest expressions of the moreu are prayer tools called inaw, whose shaved bark curled into spiraling forms. Among the Ainu, men and women held distinct craft roles, each with its own sacred traditions, and the making of inaw belonged to men. But the spiral did not stay on the prayer stick.

Women stitched the moreu in contrasting thread on ceremonial robes, working it into bands of embroidery that ran down sleeves and hems. Men cut it into knife sheaths and headbands. The same shape, repeated across every material the Ainu touched, became something larger than decoration — it became a shared language of identity, a declaration that the world spirals back toward itself and nothing ends in a straight line.

In 1869, Japan annexed Hokkaido. The Japanese government seized Ainu land and redistributed it to settlers, revoked traditional hunting and fishing rights, and forced children into Japanese-language schools where speaking Ainu meant punishment. The great bear ceremony, Iyomante, was suppressed over decades and formally restricted by local authorities in the 1950s. Artifacts were confiscated — some destroyed, others shipped to foreign museums where they were stripped of their cultural context and catalogued without their Ainu names.

Through assimilation laws and the suppression of Indigenous customs, Japanese government policy threatened the survival of Ainu traditions. UNESCO classifies the Ainu language as critically endangered, with very few fluent first-language speakers remaining. Settlers took the traditional hunting grounds, and ceremonies retreated underground — but the Ainu did not vanish. Since the 1990s, communities have been actively revitalizing their language, ceremonies, and crafts, working to restore what was nearly lost.

And here is what even the worst suppression could not do: it could not reach inside a hand. An Ainu woman with thread can still stitch the moreu onto cloth, and a man with a blade can still shave the old curls onto a fresh stick. You can burn the robe and break the prayer stick, but hands remember what objects cannot preserve.

In 2019, Japan formally recognized the Ainu as an Indigenous people. In 2020, the Upopoy National Ainu Museum opened in Hokkaido. Robes are being stitched again and prayer sticks carved, while the moreu keeps coiling upward under living fingers.

The five artifacts in this room all carry that curl — each one a different surface where the same shape took hold. The moreu is still here because hands kept making it, long after the objects themselves were taken away.


===================================================
ARTIFACT 1: INAW (Sacred Shaved Sticks · イナウ)
===================================================

Examine this pale willow stick, roughly forty centimeters long. The Ainu carver ran a blade along the bark and peeled it back — but did not cut it free. Those pale ribbons curl upward and feather outward from the shaft, each one a moreu spiral growing directly out of the wood. The form of the shavings varied by purpose and recipient: different curl patterns were thought to address different kamuy, each shape a message aimed at a specific god. Ainu people placed inaw at hearths, fishing grounds, births, weddings, and graves — anywhere a prayer needed sending. When the wood softens and the ribbons drop away, the message has been received. The inaw was always meant to be temporary, a disposable vessel carrying a prayer to the gods, then released. Nineteenth-century examples from southern Hokkaido can be found in the collections of the British Museum and the Smithsonian.


===================================================
ARTIFACT 2: ATTUSH ROBE (Elm Bark Ceremonial Robe · アットゥㇱ)
===================================================

This robe was not sewn from cotton or silk — it was pulled from a tree. Ainu women harvested the inner bark of the ohyo elm, soaking and splitting the fibers until they could be woven into this stiff, golden-brown cloth. Then, with contrasting thread, they stitched moreu spirals along every edge: the collar coils tightly inward, the cuffs spiral at each wrist, and a border of interlocking curves runs the full length of the hem. These are not decorations. Each spiral is a ward against evil spirits, a line of protection sewn directly into the garment. The person who wore this robe was wrapped entirely in spiraling protection. Attush robes from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, many originating in central and southern Hokkaido, are held at institutions including the Hokkaido Museum and the Musée du quai Branly in Paris.


===================================================
ARTIFACT 3: IKUPASUY (Prayer Stick · イクパスイ)
===================================================

This flat wooden wand, carved from katsura or willow, is about the length of a forearm. Run your eye along its surface and you will find moreu spirals incised into the wood — tight curls that seem to spin outward from the grain itself. Where the inaw was a disposable message, the ikupasuy served a different role: it was a permanent mediator between the human world and the spirit world. The worshipper dipped it into sake and flicked the liquid toward the kamuy, letting the spiral-carved wand carry the offering across that boundary. Before the ceremony, the stick was passed through the rorun-puyar, the sacred eastern window reserved for the gods alone. The ikupasuy was treasured for years, even generations. Carved examples from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries survive in the collections of the Tokyo National Museum and the Peabody Museum.


===================================================
ARTIFACT 4: MAKIRI (Carved Knife · マキリ)
===================================================

Every Ainu adult carried a makiri — a short carving knife with a steel blade and a handle of carved wood or deer antler. Notice the handle: moreu spirals are incised into the surface, their grooves worn smooth by years of use. The sheath is equally detailed, sometimes carved with elaborate coils offered as courtship gifts. But the makiri's deepest significance is hidden in plain sight: this is the knife that carves the inaw. Every sacred spiral shaved into a prayer stick was made by a blade like this one. The makiri did not just carry the moreu — it made the moreu. Makiri with carved sheaths from the nineteenth century, particularly from the Hidaka and Tokachi regions, are held in the National Museum of Ethnology in Osaka and the British Museum.


===================================================
ARTIFACT 5: MATANPUSHI (Embroidered Headband · マタンプシ)
===================================================

This narrow band of cloth, worn across the forehead, is embroidered with the most personal moreu of all. Study the center front: bold spirals curl outward from the middle of the brow, their largest coils sitting directly over a place the Ainu associated with spiritual vulnerability. Ainu women — the spiritual guardians of their households — wore the matanpushi daily, believing the spirals protected the mind from malevolent forces. The thread draws on the forest too, dyed with pigments from local bark and plant roots, supplemented by traded indigo. Worn at the boundary between self and world, the matanpushi placed the moreu where it mattered most — guarding the thoughts beneath. Matanpushi from the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, primarily from Hokkaido's Saru River region, are preserved at the Upopoy National Ainu Museum and the Hokkaido Museum.
