# Artifact 1: Bear Skull — Iyomante, the Bear-Sending Ceremony

---

A bear skull mounted on a sacred *nusa* fence, surrounded by carved wooden offerings called *inau*, is not a trophy. It is a thank-you note to a god.

The Iyomante ("bear-sending") ceremony was the most elaborate ritual in Ainu life. A captured bear cub — raised by the community for one to two years, sometimes nursed by an Ainu woman — was understood to be Kim-un Kamuy, the Bear God, visiting the human world. At the ceremony's climax, the bear was ritually killed, sending its spirit home to the divine world. The skull, mounted on the *nusa*, became a permanent record of that sacred contract: humans show reverence; nature gives life in return.

The Meiji government banned Iyomante in the late 19th century, calling it "barbaric." This exhibit begins here because understanding what was banned is the only way to understand what was almost lost.
