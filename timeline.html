History & Timeline

Ten thousand years — from origin to resistance

Scroll or drag
~13,000 BCE
Jōmon Origins
The Jōmon people — ancestors of the Ainu — inhabit the Japanese archipelago as hunter-gatherers, producing some of the world's earliest pottery. On the northern island of Hokkaido, communities remain isolated from the cultural mixing occurring farther south.
~300 BCE – 700 CE
The Yayoi Migration
Jōmon communities on Honshu mixed with the Yayoi people from the Korean Peninsula. The northern Jōmon remained isolated — no rice paddies, no bronze. Fishing, foraging, hunting. The old ways held.
7th – 13th Century
Satsumon & Okhotsk Merge
The Satsumon culture (descended from northern Jōmon) fused with the Okhotsk culture arriving from Sakhalin. From this merger, the Ainu emerged as a distinct people — their own language, spiritual practices, and material culture rooted in the forests and rivers of Hokkaido.
Pre-Contact
Life in the Forest
For thousands of years, the Ainu lived as hunter-gatherers — hunting bears and deer, fishing for salmon, gathering shellfish. They operated as middlemen traders across the Sea of Okhotsk, moving goods between Honshu, Sakhalin, and the Kurils.
1600s
The Matsumae Clan
Japanese influence tightened with the Matsumae clan's trading outpost in southern Hokkaido. The clan controlled rice, sake, iron, and cotton — demanding furs and fish in return through coercive, unequal trade.
1669
Shakushain's Revolt
Chief Shakushain united Ainu communities across Hokkaido in armed revolt against their trade overlords. The uprising was crushed through treachery — Shakushain was killed during what were supposed to be peace negotiations.
1789
Kunashiri-Menashi Revolt
A second major rebellion erupted under an even crueler regime. It failed due to internal betrayals. By the end of the 18th century, the Ainu had lost control over almost all of their ancestral territory.
1869
Meiji Annexation
The Meiji government officially annexed Hokkaido, renaming it from Ezo — the Ainu homeland. A systematic colonization campaign began. Land was seized and redistributed to Japanese settlers. Ainu had fought for generations; now it was taken by decree.
1899
Former Aborigines Protection Act
Japan forced assimilation. Ainu children were sent to Japanese-language schools where speaking Ainu meant punishment. Traditional practices were banned. The law's very title — "Former Aborigines" — declared the Ainu already gone.
1955
Iyomante Banned
The sacred bear-sending ceremony Iyomante — one of the most important Ainu spiritual practices — was banned by authorities. Artifacts were confiscated, some destroyed, others shipped to foreign museums and labeled primitive.
2008
Diet Resolution
The Japanese Diet passed a non-binding resolution recognizing the Ainu as an Indigenous people — a symbolic but significant step after over a century of denial.
2019
Ainu Promotion Act
Japan passed legislation legally recognizing the Ainu as Indigenous — 150 years after annexation. The law acknowledged historical discrimination, though critics noted it lacked land rights or self-determination provisions.
2020
Upopoy Museum Opens
The Upopoy National Ainu Museum opened in Shiraoi, Hokkaido — the first national institution dedicated to Ainu history. The spiral is still being stitched. Still being shaved. Still coiling upward from new hands.
Today
The Spiral Endures
Fewer than ten native speakers remain. The hunting grounds are gone. But Ainu communities continue to revitalize their language, practices, and arts. The moreu was never lost. It was just waiting.
