History & Timeline

Ten thousand years — from origin to resistance

~13,000 BCE
Jōmon Origins
The Jōmon people — ancestors of the Ainu — inhabit the Japanese archipelago as hunter-gatherers, producing some of the world's earliest pottery. On Hokkaido, communities remain isolated from the mixing occurring farther south.
~300 BCE – 700 CE
The Yayoi Migration
Jōmon communities on Honshu mixed with the Yayoi from the Korean Peninsula. The north held to older ways — no rice paddies, no bronze. Fishing, foraging, hunting.
7th – 13th Century
Satsumon & Okhotsk Merge
The Satsumon culture fused with the Okhotsk culture from Sakhalin. From this merger, the Ainu emerged — their own language, spiritual practices, and material culture rooted in the forests of Hokkaido.
Pre-Contact
Life in the Forest
For thousands of years, the Ainu lived as hunter-gatherers — hunting bears and deer, fishing for salmon. They operated as middlemen traders across the Sea of Okhotsk, moving goods between Honshu, Sakhalin, and the Kurils.
1600s
The Matsumae Clan
Japanese influence tightened with the Matsumae clan's trading outpost in southern Hokkaido — controlling rice, sake, iron, cotton — demanding furs and fish through coercive, unequal trade.
1669
Shakushain's Revolt
Chief Shakushain united Ainu communities in armed revolt. The uprising was crushed through treachery — Shakushain killed during what were supposed to be peace negotiations.
1789
Kunashiri-Menashi Revolt
A second major rebellion under an even crueler regime. It failed due to internal betrayals. By the end of the 18th century, the Ainu had lost almost all ancestral territory.
1869
Meiji Annexation
The Meiji government annexed Hokkaido, renaming it from Ezo. A systematic colonization campaign began. Ainu land was seized and redistributed to Japanese settlers.
1899
Former Aborigines Protection Act
Forced assimilation. Children sent to Japanese schools — speaking Ainu meant punishment. Traditional practices banned. The law's title declared the Ainu already gone.
1955
Iyomante Banned
The sacred bear-sending ceremony Iyomante was banned. Artifacts confiscated, some destroyed, others shipped to foreign museums labeled primitive and stripped of their names.
2008
Diet Resolution
The Japanese Diet passed a non-binding resolution recognizing the Ainu as Indigenous — symbolic but significant after a century of denial.
2019
Ainu Promotion Act
Japan legally recognized the Ainu as Indigenous — 150 years late. The law acknowledged discrimination, though critics noted it lacked land rights or self-determination.
2020
Upopoy Museum Opens
The Upopoy National Ainu Museum opened in Shiraoi — the first national institution for Ainu history. The spiral is still being stitched. Still coiling upward from new hands.
Today
The Spiral Endures
Fewer than ten native speakers remain. But the moreu — the sacred spiral — was never lost. It was just waiting.
