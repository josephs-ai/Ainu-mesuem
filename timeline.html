History & Timeline

The Ainu — from origin to the forest

~13,000 BCE
Jōmon Origins
The Ainu are the Indigenous people of Hokkaido, the large northern island of Japan. Their ancestors — the Jōmon — inhabited the archipelago as hunter-gatherers for thousands of years, producing some of the world's earliest pottery. On Hokkaido, communities remained isolated from the cultural mixing occurring farther south on Honshu.
Inaw prayer stick · Peabody Museum
~300 BCE – 700 CE
The Yayoi Migration
When Jōmon communities on Honshu began to mix with the Yayoi people from the Korean Peninsula, the northern Jōmon communities remained isolated. Wet-rice agriculture and bronze tools transformed the south, but the north held to older ways — fishing, foraging, hunting.
7th – 13th Century
Satsumon & Okhotsk Merge
The Satsumon culture, descended from northern Jōmon, merged with the Okhotsk culture arriving from Sakhalin and the Sea of Okhotsk coast. From this fusion, the Ainu emerged as a distinct people — with their own language, spiritual practices, and material culture rooted in the forests and rivers of Hokkaido.
Attush bark-cloth robe · Seattle Art Museum
Pre-Contact Era
Life in the Forest
For thousands of years, they lived in deep forest — hunting bear and deer, fishing rivers choked with salmon, reading the land the way others read books. They didn't divide the world into sacred and ordinary. To them, everything alive carried a spirit, a kamuy: the fire, the water, the bear, the owl, the mountain. The forest wasn't scenery. It was congregation. Every tree a god standing still.
Ikupasuy prayer wand · AMNH
Pre-Contact Era
The Middlemen Traders
Trade was central to their world: they operated as middlemen across the Sea of Okhotsk, moving goods between Honshu to the south and Sakhalin and the Kurils to the north and northeast. Their position between worlds — geographic and spiritual — defined them.
Timeless
The Moreu Spiral
When an Ainu craftsman made a prayer tool, he started with a stick. He didn't carve it into a new shape — he shaved it, peeling back thin curls of wood so they spiraled upward from the shaft like smoke rising, or like a fern uncurling in spring. These were inaw — prayer sticks — and the shavings didn't fall away. They stayed attached, coiling outward, becoming the prayer itself. Wood becoming curl. Material becoming spirit.
Makiri knife sheath · Peabody Museum
Timeless
The Spiral Spreads
That curl — that moreu — moved from the inaw onto everything the Ainu touched. Women stitched it in white thread on dark robes, working the spiral into bands of embroidery that ran down sleeves and hems. Men cut it into knife sheaths and headbands. The same shape, repeated until it was no longer decoration. It was language. It was identity. It was proof that the world spirals back toward itself, that nothing ends in a straight line.
Matanpushi headband · Brooklyn Museum
1600s
The Matsumae Clan
Japanese influence tightened with the establishment of the Matsumae clan's trading outpost in southern Hokkaido. The clan controlled access to rice, sake, iron, and cotton — demanding furs and fish in return, exploiting the Ainu through unequal trade that grew more coercive with each decade.
1669
Shakushain's Revolt
The chief Shakushain united Ainu communities across Hokkaido to lead a violent revolt against their trade overlords. The uprising was crushed through treachery — Shakushain was killed during what were supposed to be peace negotiations.
1789
Kunashiri-Menashi Revolt
A second major rebellion erupted at Kunashiri-Menashi under an even more cruel regime. It also failed, this time due to internal betrayals. By the end of the 18th century, the Ainu had lost control over almost all of their ancestral territory.
1869
Meiji Annexation
In 1869, Japan annexed Hokkaido. What followed was systematic erasure. Ainu land was seized and redistributed to Japanese settlers. Children were sent to schools where speaking Ainu meant punishment. The great bear ceremony, Iomante, was banned in 1955. Artifacts were confiscated. Some were destroyed. Some ended up in foreign museums, labeled primitive and exotic, stripped of their names.
20th Century
Near Extinction
Japan tried to make the Ainu disappear. And it nearly worked. Today, fewer than ten native speakers of the Ainu language remain. The traditional hunting grounds are gone. The ceremonies are gone. The bear rituals are gone. By almost every measure used by linguists and anthropologists, the Ainu are one of the closest cases of cultural extinction in the modern world.
Ainu robe · The Metropolitan Museum of Art
Still
What Extinction Cannot Do
But here is what cultural extinction cannot do: it cannot reach inside a hand. Any Ainu woman with a needle and thread can stitch the moreu onto a new cloth. Any Ainu man with a blade can shave a fresh curl onto a plain stick. You can burn the robe. You can break the prayer stick. You can clear-cut the forest until there is nothing left but stumps. But you cannot confiscate muscle memory. You cannot arrest the knowledge that lives in fingers. The moreu survived because it never lived in objects. It lived in people.
