History & Timeline

Ten thousand years of the Ainu, from origin to resistance

Scroll
~13,000 BCE
Jōmon Origins
The Jōmon people — ancestors of the Ainu — inhabit the Japanese archipelago as hunter-gatherers, producing some of the world's earliest pottery. On the northern island of Hokkaido, communities remain isolated from the cultural mixing occurring farther south on Honshu.
~300 BCE – 700 CE
The Yayoi Migration
When Jōmon communities on Honshu began to mix with the Yayoi people from the Korean Peninsula, the northern Jōmon communities remained isolated. Wet-rice agriculture and bronze tools transformed the south, but the north held to older ways — fishing, foraging, hunting.
7th – 13th Century
The Satsumon & Okhotsk Merge
The Satsumon culture (descended from northern Jōmon) merged with the Okhotsk culture arriving from Sakhalin and the Sea of Okhotsk coast. From this fusion, the Ainu emerged as a distinct people — with their own language, spiritual practices, and material culture rooted in the forests and rivers of Hokkaido.
Pre-Contact Era
Life in the Forest
For thousands of years, the Ainu lived as hunter-gatherers in the dense forests of Hokkaido — hunting bears and deer, fishing for salmon, gathering shellfish along the coast. Trade was central to their world: they operated as middlemen across the Sea of Okhotsk, moving goods between Honshu to the south and Sakhalin and the Kurils to the north and northeast.
1600s
The Matsumae Clan
Japanese influence tightened with the establishment of the Matsumae clan's trading outpost in southern Hokkaido. The clan controlled access to rice, sake, iron, and cotton — demanding furs and fish in return, exploiting the Ainu through unequal trade that grew more coercive with each decade.
1669
Shakushain's Revolt
The chief Shakushain united Ainu communities across Hokkaido to lead a violent revolt against their trade overlords. The uprising was crushed through treachery — Shakushain was killed during what were supposed to be peace negotiations. The first organized resistance, and the first betrayal.
1789
Kunashiri-Menashi Revolt
A second major rebellion erupted at Kunashiri-Menashi under an even more cruel regime. It also failed, this time due to internal betrayals. By the end of the 18th century, the Ainu had lost control over almost all of their ancestral territory.
1869
Meiji Annexation of Hokkaido
The Meiji government officially annexed Hokkaido, renaming it from Ezo — the Ainu homeland — and launching a systematic colonization campaign. The Ainu had already fought for generations for their land. Now it was taken by decree.
1899
Former Aborigines Protection Act
Japan passed the "Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act," forcibly assimilating the Ainu. Ainu children were sent to Japanese-language schools where speaking Ainu meant punishment. Traditional practices were discouraged or banned. Ainu land was redistributed to Japanese settlers.
1955
Iyomante Banned
The Iyomante — the sacred bear-sending ceremony, one of the most important Ainu spiritual practices — was banned by authorities. Artifacts were confiscated. Some were destroyed. Others ended up in foreign museums, labeled primitive and exotic, stripped of their Ainu names.
2008
Indigenous Recognition (Diet Resolution)
The Japanese Diet passed a non-binding resolution recognizing the Ainu as an Indigenous people of Japan — a symbolic but significant first step after over a century of denial.
2019
Ainu Promotion Act
Japan finally passed legislation legally recognizing the Ainu as Indigenous — 150 years after annexation. The law acknowledged historical discrimination and promoted Ainu culture, though critics noted it lacked land rights or self-determination provisions.
2020
Upopoy National Ainu Museum Opens
The Upopoy National Ainu Museum and Park opened in Shiraoi, Hokkaido — the first national institution dedicated to Ainu history and culture. The spiral is still being stitched. Still being shaved. Still coiling upward from new hands into new cloth.
Today
The Spiral Endures
Fewer than ten native speakers of the Ainu language remain. The traditional hunting grounds are gone. But Ainu communities continue efforts to revitalize their language, practices, and arts. The moreu — the sacred spiral — was never lost. It was just waiting.
